Modern neuroscience complicates that picture. Memory depends on distributed networks, reconstruction, association, timing, sensory traces, affective state, and embodied context. The FHQCM begins from this distributed view. Memory is not treated as a static object retrieved unchanged from storage. It is treated as a recursive reconstruction in which the present reassembles the past through attention, emotion, pattern recognition, and meaning. This is why memory can heal, distort, deepen, fragment, or become integrated. Within the model, memory becomes one of the clearest human examples of whole-in-part patterning. A smell, a sound, a phrase, a dream, or a bodily sensation can call forth a wider field of remembered relationship. A small fragment may activate a whole world. Pribram and the Holonomic Analogy Karl Pribram's holonomic brain theory explored the possibility that aspects of perception and memory may be understood through holographic or Fourier-like principles of distributed patterning pribram1991Brain. Whether or not every strong version of that theory survives scientific scrutiny, it remains important for FHQCM because it offers a serious vocabulary for thinking about distributed information rather than isolated storage.
