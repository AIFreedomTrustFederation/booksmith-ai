\chapter{Coincidence, Coherence, and the Eternal Now}

\section{Introduction}

What is a coincidence?

In ordinary language, a coincidence is the occurrence of two or more events that appear meaningfully related yet lack an immediately obvious causal connection. The term derives from the Latin \emph{coincidere}, meaning ``to fall together.'' Events are said to coincide when they arrive at the same place, time, or meaning.

Yet this common definition presupposes separation. It assumes that events normally exist apart and only occasionally intersect.

This chapter explores an alternative hypothesis: coincidence is not the exception but the disclosure of a deeper coherence already present within the structure of reality.

\[

This is an FHQCM formal definition, not an established scientific law.

\section{Critiques and Alternative Explanations}

A rigorous account of coincidence must include competing explanations.

Statistical coincidence: many events happen, so rare alignments are inevitable.

Apophenia: humans detect patterns that may not objectively exist.

Confirmation bias: people remember meaningful coincidences and forget ordinary non-events.

Predictive processing: the brain constructs meaning through expectation and interpretation.

Jungian synchronicity: meaningful coincidence may occur without direct causation.

FHQCM interpretation: coincidence reflects observer recognition of participation within $\Omega$.

\section{What Would Weaken This Hypothesis?}

The Relational Coincidence Hypothesis would be weakened if coincidence experiences cannot be distinguished from statistical clustering, confirmation bias, apophenia, or ordinary predictive processing.

It would also be weakened if relational participation cannot be operationalized in any meaningful way.
